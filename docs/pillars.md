# Design Pillars

Three commitments. Every mechanic, room, weapon, and encounter is measured against them. If a feature doesn't serve at least one pillar — and doesn't fight the others — it doesn't ship.

## 01 · Tension through escalation

The game never lets a space become safe. Rooms don't exist until Ariadna enters them, and each one tracks its own level that rises on every visit. Enemies, hazards, and room behavior scale with that level, so **familiarity is a trap, not a comfort**.

- The player is always trading known danger for unknown reward.
- Backtracking is a real decision, not a free reset.
- The boss escalates on a separate, never-resetting track — the pressure only ever climbs.

## 02 · Crafted power through modular guns

Power is something the player **builds**, not something they pick up. Guns are assembled from parts on a workbench, with stats that update live as pieces snap together.

- A Body and a Handle are mandatory; Barrel, Stock, and Sight are optional trade-offs.
- The fantasy is *ritual craftsmanship* — metalwork, focused light, negative space — not a loot dropdown.
- Every build is a readable set of choices: damage vs. accuracy vs. recoil vs. range.

## 03 · Psychological weight

The horror is authored, not random. The labyrinth is a projection of Ariadna's trauma, and the systems exist to make that legible.

- **Rooms** shift from cold and clinical toward oppressive and surreal.
- **Enemies** and the **boss** embody her father rather than generic monsters.
- **Non-Euclidean navigation** (color-coded portals) keeps the space psychologically unstable.

---

!!! note "Reading a pillar conflict"
    When two pillars pull against each other — e.g. a satisfying gun build that trivializes escalation — escalation wins. The horror is the product; power is the tool that makes surviving it feel earned.
